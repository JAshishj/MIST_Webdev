Famous foods of Karnataka

Bisi Bele Bhath :-

Bisi Bele Bahth is one of Karnataka's most iconic dishes, literally translating to "Hot Lentil Rice." It is a wholesome one-pot meal made with rice, toor dal (pigeon peas), tamarind, jaggery, and a special spice mix called Bisi Bele Bhath Powder. The dish has a perfect balance of spicy, tangy, and slightly sweet flavors, making it unique from regular sambar rice. Often served hot with ghee, boondi, or papad, it is a comfort food loved across the state. Traditionally, it is prepared during festivals and family gatherings, but it has also become a common dish in hotels and homes alike. Recipe of Bisi Bele Bath

Neer Dosa :-

Neer Dosa is a soft, lace-like dosa made from a watery rice batter, native to coastal Karnataka. Unlike regular dosa, it does not require fermentation, making it quick and easy to prepare. The word "Neer" means water in Kannada, referring to the thin consistency of the batter. These dosas are light, soft, and melt in the mouth. They are usually served with coconut chutney, vegetable sagu, chicken curry, or fish curry. Because of their light texture, they are ideal for breakfast or dinner. Neer Dosa reflects the simple yet flavorful food culture of the coastal belt of Karnataka. Recipe of Neer Dosa

Masala Dosa :-

Masala Dosa is perhaps the most recognized South Indian dish across the world, and Karnataka has played a big role in popularizing it. It is a thin, crispy crepe made from fermented rice and urad dal batter. The dosa is usually filled with a spiced potato and onion curry, which adds flavor and texture. Served hot with coconut chutney, tomato chutney, and sambar, it makes for a wholesome breakfast or dinner. While there are many varieties of dosa, the masala dosa stands out for its balance of crispness on the outside and softness on the inside. Originating in Udupi, it has become a symbol of Karnataka's vegetarian cuisine. Recipe of Masala Dosa

Akki Rotti :-

Akki Rotti is a traditional Karnataka dish made from rice flour, often mixed with onions, green chilies, coriander, and grated vegetables like carrots. The dough is patted thin directly on a hot griddle or banana leaf and then roasted until golden brown. It is typically enjoyed with coconut chutney or a spicy chutney powder, and sometimes paired with curd. Akki Rotti is a staple breakfast in many rural and urban households because it is both filling and nutritious. Its name literally means “rice bread,” and it reflects the heavy use of rice in Karnataka's cuisine, particularly in regions like Bangalore and Mysore. Recipe of Akki Rotti

Kesari Bath :-

Kesari Bhath is a delicious sweet dish prepared from semolina (rava), sugar, ghee, and flavored with cardamom. It gets its name from “Kesari,” meaning saffron, which gives the dish its golden-yellow color. Often garnished with cashews and raisins fried in ghee, this melt-in-the-mouth dessert is popular during festivals, functions, and as a special breakfast treat. It is also a common offering in temples. In Karnataka, Kesari Bhath is often paired with Khara Bhath (a savory upma) and served together as “Chow Chow Bath,” giving a mix of both sweet and spicy flavors on one plate. Its rich taste makes it a favorite across age groups. Recipe of Kesari Bath

Mangalore Buns :-

Mangalore Buns are a unique breakfast or tea-time snack originating from the coastal city of Mangalore. Despite the name, they are not actual buns but soft, slightly sweet, deep-fried pooris made with mashed bananas, flour, sugar, and curd. They puff up beautifully when fried and have a fluffy inside with a mild sweetness. They are usually served with coconut chutney or vegetable curry, but many enjoy them plain with tea or coffee. The use of overripe bananas in the recipe not only avoids wastage but also gives the dish a distinct flavor. Mangalore Buns are a true coastal delicacy and showcase the creative side of Karnataka's cuisine. Recipe of Mangalore Buns

Goli Baje :-

Goli Baje, also called Mangalore Bajji, is a popular snack from coastal Karnataka. These are soft, fluffy fritters made from maida (refined flour), yogurt, green chilies, ginger, and curry leaves. The batter is deep-fried into round balls, which are crispy on the outside and soft inside. Goli Baje is often served hot with coconut chutney and is a staple evening snack in Mangalore households and restaurants. Their slightly tangy taste, thanks to the yogurt fermentation, makes them addictive. Usually enjoyed with tea or coffee, Goli Baje reflects the coastal love for fried, flavorful snacks that bring families together in the evenings. Recipe of Goli Baje

Chow Chow Bath :-

Chow Chow Bhath is a classic Karnataka breakfast that offers both sweet and savory flavors on a single plate. It consists of two dishes served together: Khara Bhath (a spicy upma made with semolina, vegetables, and spices) and Kesari Bhath (a sweet semolina dish flavored with saffron or cardamom). The combination provides a contrast of tastes — spicy and tangy from the Khara Bath, and sweet and rich from the Kesari Bath. This pairing is widely enjoyed in Bangalore eateries and is often considered the perfect breakfast for those who can't decide between sweet and savory. It beautifully showcases Karnataka's balance of flavors. Recipe of Chow Chow Bath

Pundi :-

Pundi, also known as rice dumplings, is a traditional dish from the Mangalore and Udupi regions. It is made by steaming rice balls prepared from ground rice and a light tempering of curry leaves, mustard seeds, and grated coconut. Pundi has a soft and chewy texture and is typically eaten with coconut chutney or a spicy curry. In many households, it is served as a filling breakfast or dinner option. Since it is steamed, it is considered healthy and light on the stomach. Pundi is a simple yet satisfying dish that reflects the coastal emphasis on rice-based preparations. Recipe of Pundi

Kadle Avalakki :-

Kadle Avalakki is a light and healthy snack made from flattened rice (poha) and kadle (chickpeas or Bengal gram). It is a popular evening snack in Karnataka, especially in the coastal belt. The dish is prepared by mixing poha with grated coconut, chopped onions, chilies, curry leaves, and roasted chickpeas, often seasoned with a light tempering of mustard seeds and green chilies. Kadle Avalakki is not deep-fried, making it a wholesome alternative to oily snacks. It is often served with tea and is loved for its crunchy and soft texture combination. his dish is proof that simple ingredients can create memorable flavors. Recipe of Kadle Avalakki

Idli Vada :-

Idli Vada is the ultimate South Indian breakfast combo, and it holds a special place in Karnataka's food culture. Idlis are soft, steamed rice cakes made from fermented rice and urad dal batter, while Vadas are deep-fried savory doughnuts made from urad dal, flavored with onions, chilies, and curry leaves. Together, they are served with coconut chutney and a bowl of hot sambar. The combination is both filling and nutritious, offering a balance of carbohydrates and proteins. Idli Vada is not just food, but a tradition in Karnataka, served in homes, restaurants, and even during festivals. It continues to symbolize simplicity and flavor. Recipe of Idli & Recipe of Vada
